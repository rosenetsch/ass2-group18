Help event creators build respectful and meaningful Acknowledgement to Country statements that honour the Traditional Custodians of the land and support reconciliation.

Understanding the difference

AoC vs Welcome to Country

Acknowledgement to Country
  • Can be spoken by anyone
  • Used at the start of meetings and events
  • Shows respect to Traditional Custodians
  • Promotes reconciliation by recognising the enduring connection of First Nations peoples to their Country
Welcome to Country
  • Performed by Elders or Traditional Custodians only
  • Formal welcome onto Country
  • May include ceremonies and performances
  • Must be arranged with the community in advance

Be aware

Common Mistakes and Ethical Considerations

Copying without research

Don't copy acknowledgements without researching the correct Traditional Custodians for your specific location.

Using incorrect Nation names

Always verify the correct name of the Traditional Custodians for the land where your event is held.

Confusing AoC with Welcome to Country

These are different practices. Only Elders or Traditional Custodians can perform a Welcome to Country.

Tokenistic box-ticking

An acknowledgement should be genuine and meaningful, not just a formality to check off before an event.

Culturally inappropriate wording

Avoid language that is inaccurate, generalised or disrespectful of the culture being acknowledged.
